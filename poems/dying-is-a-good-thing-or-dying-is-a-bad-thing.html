Dying is a good thing or dying is a bad thing

The picture frame was not even tapped into the wall
with a couple of well-placed hammer blows;
it was guided, carefully, hung on plastic tip pushpins
for cork boards, the trailer walls were so soft.

A baby on a tractor with big tires,
a red one being steered by his grandfather,
whose face now’s dusty (and that’s wiped off)
as a thumbprint’s been in their faces.

The grandfather’s smiling and wearing glasses
that aren’t sharp—not even orthogonal,
more rounded and oval, and they sit on his face,
which is smiling, holding the baby who’s pointing

toward someone not the cameraperson,
wearing a onesie with teddy bears on it:
they’re plopped down there incongruous but cute,
red but not tractor red, fire hydrant red, China red.

Clay like the fields, more like, which another man
now gets out and tills, in a little sunhat, sunglasses
that blacken everything, like memory fading black
without the moon to catch it all slipping away.
